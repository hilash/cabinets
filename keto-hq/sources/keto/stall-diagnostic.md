# Keto Stall Diagnostic Checklist

## Claim
Fat-loss plateaus lasting 2–3 weeks are **normal and expected** within a sustained keto or any caloric-deficit protocol. A structured diagnostic separates expected physiological plateaus from correctable errors in adherence or programming. Work the list in order before changing macros.

## Evidence tier
Clinical / applied consensus from nutrition and sports practitioners (Lyle McDonald's body-recomposition work, Volek & Phinney, Eric Helms) plus physiological studies on energy balance and adaptive thermogenesis. **Emerging** because the diagnostic *sequence* is clinical judgment rather than RCT-validated; the underlying mechanisms (adaptive thermogenesis, water-flux / "whoosh," cortisol-mediated retention) each have strong evidence individually.

## The checklist — run in order

### 1. Recheck calorie math
- Measured vs eyeballed portions drift ~20–30% in untrained loggers.
- Cooking oil, nut butters, cheese, cream are the most-underestimated items.
- **Test:** weigh everything for 5 days. If intake was higher than tracked, the stall is explained. Adjust deficit modestly (50–150 kcal), not drastically.

### 2. Hidden carbs
- Sauces, salad dressings, "keto" branded products, dairy (lactose), nut butters (watch sugar alcohols and total carbs), rotating artificial sweeteners with GI distress can mask adherence issues.
- **Test:** 5 days with only unprocessed foods and standard condiments (olive oil, vinegar, salt, herbs).

### 3. Electrolyte status → water-weight rebound
- After a sodium correction, it's common to see a 1–3 lb scale rebound that isn't fat regain.
- **Test:** track 7-day rolling average, not daily weight.

### 4. "Whoosh" timing
- Fat cells can temporarily fill with water post-lipolysis, masking the loss. Release (a "whoosh") typically happens within 1–3 weeks of steady adherence.
- **Test:** hold protocol steady. If adherence and calories check out, the plateau usually resolves.
- Evidence base: applied observation; not well-formalized in literature but widely described.

### 5. Sleep & stress (cortisol-mediated retention)
- Sleep debt elevates cortisol, which promotes glycogen-bound water retention and appetite dysregulation.
- Chronic life stress does the same.
- **Test:** quantify — any nights <6h in past week? stress event? If yes, baseline these before blaming diet.

### 6. Menstrual cycle (if applicable)
- Late luteal water retention (3–5 days before menses) can mask 1–3 lb of fat loss.
- **Test:** align scale comparisons across cycle phase, not calendar weeks.

### 7. Only if 1–6 are clean: metabolic adaptation
- Sustained deficits lower maintenance expenditure ~5–15% beyond mass loss alone.
- **Response:** a short (1–2 week) "diet break" at maintenance calories can restore responsiveness before resuming the deficit.
- Evidence: **Trexler, Smith-Ryan, Norton — "Metabolic adaptation to weight loss: implications for the athlete" (J Int Soc Sports Nutr 2014).** Strong review.

## What NOT to do first

- **Don't cut calories further** until 1–6 are audited. Further restriction into a real adaptation stall worsens it.
- **Don't add exercise** as the first lever — it compounds stress if sleep/life stress is driving the stall.
- **Don't add exogenous ketones.** They raise BHB without changing metabolic context. No evidence for fat-loss benefit from exogenous ketones. Skip.

## Adherence is the biggest variable

Across studies, adherence explains more variance in outcome than dietary composition. A stall is often the protocol meeting the reality of life, not a failure of the protocol.
