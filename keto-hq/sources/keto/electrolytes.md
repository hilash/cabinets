# Electrolytes on a Ketogenic Diet

## Claim
Ketogenic eating lowers insulin, which reduces sodium reabsorption at the kidney. This natriuresis pulls potassium and magnesium along with it. Replacing these three minerals at appropriate levels prevents most "keto flu" symptoms (headache, fatigue, muscle cramps, lightheadedness on standing, palpitations) and supports sustained adherence.

## Mechanism
- Insulin promotes renal sodium retention. Low-insulin state → increased sodium excretion.
- Sodium excretion drives obligate water loss (first-week "water weight" drop).
- Potassium and magnesium losses follow sodium — partly cellular redistribution, partly renal.
- Classical reference: Phinney, Bistrian et al. — metabolic ward studies of protein-sparing modified fasts demonstrated sodium requirements rise during ketogenic adaptation.

## Daily targets (standard adult, no contraindications)

- **Sodium:** 3,000–5,000 mg/day total (food + added salt + broth). Active / hot-climate / sweating days: higher end.
- **Potassium:** 3,500–4,700 mg/day from **food** (AI per IOM is ~4,700 mg). Supplementation is restricted OTC to ~99 mg/pill for safety — food-based is the only practical route for most people.
- **Magnesium:** ~400 mg elemental/day. Food ± supplement. See [[../../personal-os/health/sources/supplements/magnesium-glycinate]] when running alongside Personal OS.

## Evidence
- **Phinney SD (Nutr Metab 2004) — Ketogenic diets and physical performance.** Classic review with the sodium-loss mechanism and empirical targets.
- **Volek & Phinney — Art and Science of Low Carbohydrate Living (2011).** Applied electrolyte framework.
- **IOM (2005) — Dietary Reference Intakes for Water, Potassium, Sodium, Chloride, and Sulfate.** Reference physiological values, ULs. https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/10925
- **NIH Office of Dietary Supplements — Magnesium Fact Sheet, Potassium Fact Sheet.** Reference framework for supplementation ranges and ULs.

## Practical food sources

### Sodium
- Table salt (2,300 mg per teaspoon). A pinch at each meal is meaningful.
- Salted broth (bone broth, bouillon) — 500–1,000 mg per cup.
- "Sole water" (saturated salt solution — a few ml at a time).
- Olives, cured meats (within protein targets).

### Potassium
- Avocado (~700 mg per fruit)
- Spinach, Swiss chard (cooked volumes deliver high K)
- Salmon (~600 mg per 6 oz fillet)
- Mushrooms (cooked)
- Tomato (watch net carbs)

### Magnesium
- Pumpkin seeds (~150 mg per oz)
- Almonds (~80 mg per oz)
- Dark chocolate 85%+ (within carb budget)
- Leafy greens
- Supplement glycinate or citrate to close the gap

## Hard rules

- **Blood pressure medications:** sodium + potassium targets standard above do NOT apply. Defer to prescriber.
- **Renal impairment (eGFR <60):** Both potassium and magnesium require physician input. Risk of hyperkalemia / hypermagnesemia.
- **Heart failure:** sodium targets are highly individual; defer to clinical team.
- **Potassium supplements >99 mg/pill:** prescription-only for safety. Do not recommend food-equivalence guesses that exceed this.
- **SGLT2 inhibitors:** sodium-losing by mechanism — combined with keto elevates electrolyte risks; coordinate with prescriber.

## Symptom → mineral mapping (starting points, not diagnostic)

| Symptom | First-line suspicion |
|---------|---------------------|
| Headache + fatigue in first week | Sodium |
| Muscle cramps (calves at night) | Magnesium ± sodium |
| Lightheaded on standing | Sodium ± volume |
| Heart palpitations | Sodium ± potassium — if persistent, evaluate clinically |
| Constipation | Magnesium + fiber + water |
| Persistent weakness / brain fog after week 2 | Re-check all three; rule out inadequate calories/protein |

## Hydration coupling

Water alone during an electrolyte-losing state worsens dilutional deficits. Water + electrolytes together.
