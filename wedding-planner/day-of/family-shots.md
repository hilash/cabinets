# 📸 Family + Wedding-Party Photo Shotlist

Family portraits are the most common day-of bottleneck. A 30-minute window can balloon to 90 minutes if the shotlist isn't pre-built and people aren't pre-warned.

## How to build the list

The photographer will shoot what you ask for. They will NOT know who's who. **You assign every shot in advance.**

Build the list as named groupings, in shot order. Group similar people so they're not running around.

## Standard shot order (~30 min total)

> Adjust headcounts based on family realities. Designate one person per side as the "family wrangler" — they call names and physically move people to the right spot.

1. **Couple alone** (5 min) — usually before family arrives
2. **Couple + officiant** (1 shot) — usually right after ceremony
3. **Couple + bride's immediate family** (parents + siblings + their partners) (5 min)
4. **Couple + bride's extended family** (grandparents, key aunts/uncles) (5 min)
5. **Couple + groom's immediate family** (5 min)
6. **Couple + groom's extended family** (5 min)
7. **Couple + both families combined** (1 shot)
8. **Couple + bridal party** (5 min — small groups + big group)
9. **Couple + groomsmen** (5 min)
10. **Couple + full wedding party combined** (1 shot)

## Naming the shots in advance

Create the list in this format. The photographer + family wrangler use it on the day:

```
Group 1: Couple + bride's parents
  - Bride
  - Groom
  - Bride's mom: [name]
  - Bride's dad: [name]

Group 2: Couple + bride's parents + siblings
  - Bride
  - Groom
  - Bride's mom: [name]
  - Bride's dad: [name]
  - Sibling 1: [name]
  - Sibling 2: [name]
  - Sibling 1's partner: [name] (if including)

[etc.]
```

## The "family-wrangler" role

The single most useful unsung role at any wedding. Pick someone NOT in the wedding party — usually a cousin, family friend, or aunt who knows everyone on one side.

Their job:
1. Stand next to the photographer with the printed shotlist.
2. Call out the next group by name: "Sarah's mom and dad next, please!"
3. Physically corral people who wander off (uncle Jim is always at the bar).
4. Cross off shots as they're taken.
5. Tell the photographer when a shot is missing someone vs when it's done.

Pick one wrangler per side. Tell them their role at the rehearsal. Hand them the printed list in the morning.

## Sensitive situations

- **Divorced parents.** Don't put them next to each other in shots unless they're fine with it. Have separate shots: couple+mom, couple+dad. Combined family shots either include both or skip — discuss with the parents in advance.
- **Deceased family.** Some couples include a portrait or empty chair as a tribute. Some don't. Either is fine, but decide before the day so the photographer doesn't ask in the moment.
- **Estranged relatives.** Skip them. The wedding photographer is not a reconciliation event.
- **Children.** Schedule kid shots early in the photo block — before they're tired, hungry, or have bolted.

## What the photographer does NOT need

- A list of "candid moments to capture" — they do this on instinct and you'll get more by trusting them.
- Pinterest mood boards of poses. They have their own style; you hired them for it.
- A list of every guest. Family + wedding party only. Guest portraits happen organically during cocktail + reception.

## After the wedding

The photographer typically delivers in 4–8 weeks. Ask for the family-shots subset to be flagged in the gallery — for thank-you cards, for parents' frames, etc. Most photographers will do this if asked.
