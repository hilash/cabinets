# Ferritin Reference Ranges

## Claim
Serum ferritin is the standard marker of iron stores. Standard lab ranges (often starting around 10–15 ng/mL for women, 20–30 for men on the low end) reflect population distributions, not functional sufficiency. Many experts target ferritin **>50 ng/mL** for adequate iron stores in the absence of inflammation — particularly for individuals with symptoms of iron insufficiency (fatigue, hair shedding, low exercise tolerance, restless legs) despite "in range" labs.

## Mechanism
Ferritin is the intracellular iron storage protein; serum ferritin is a small fraction that roughly correlates with whole-body iron stores. It's also an acute-phase reactant — inflammation raises it independently of iron status, which is why it must be interpreted alongside CRP/hs-CRP.

## Evidence
- **WHO (2020) — Serum ferritin concentrations for the assessment of iron status in individuals and populations.** Uses 15 ng/mL as the population cutoff for deficiency in adults; acknowledges tissue iron depletion may occur at higher levels. https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-NMH-NHD-MNM-11.2
- **Camaschella C. (NEJM 2015) — Iron-Deficiency Anemia.** Reviews diagnosis including the limitations of ferritin cutoffs and role of inflammation.
- **Short & Domagalski 2013 (Am Fam Physician) — Iron Deficiency Anemia: Evaluation and Management.** Clinical review supporting the view that symptoms of iron insufficiency can occur above the classic deficiency threshold.
- **British Society of Gastroenterology guidelines (2021)** — acknowledges that ferritin <30 ng/mL is consistent with iron deficiency in adults.
- Functional medicine / sports medicine literature commonly targets ≥50 ng/mL for endurance athletes and menstruating individuals with symptoms — clinical consensus rather than RCT-derived.

## Interpretation framework

| Ferritin (ng/mL) | Interpretation (non-inflamed) |
|------------------|-------------------------------|
| <15              | Iron deficient (WHO) |
| 15–30            | Likely iron insufficient; investigate |
| 30–50            | Low-normal; symptoms can be present |
| 50–150           | Adequate for most |
| 150–300          | High-normal; consider inflammation, alcohol, metabolic syndrome |
| >300–500         | Evaluate for iron overload (hemochromatosis, inflammation, liver disease) |

**Always interpret alongside:**
- hs-CRP (inflammation can falsely elevate ferritin)
- Transferrin saturation (TSAT) — a functional marker of iron availability
- CBC (hemoglobin, MCV)
- Sex, menstrual status, pregnancy, recent blood donation, diet

## Who this framework does NOT apply to unchanged
- Active inflammatory states (infection, autoimmune flare) — ferritin will be elevated regardless of iron stores.
- Hemochromatosis and related iron-overload conditions — high ferritin is pathological.
- Pregnancy — separate targets apply.
- Children — pediatric references differ.

## Actions based on interpretation
- **Deficient or insufficient with symptoms:** discuss with physician. Oral iron therapy has specific dosing (alternate-day dosing often superior; take with vitamin C, separate from calcium/coffee/tea).
- **Adequate:** maintain dietary iron; no supplementation needed.
- **High:** investigate cause before supplementing anything iron-related.
