# Vitamin D3

## Claim
Adults with 25(OH)D below 30 ng/mL (75 nmol/L) typically benefit from supplementation with vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol), with dose titrated to achieve a blood level of 30–50 ng/mL (or per personal physician's target).

## Mechanism
Vitamin D is a prohormone. It regulates calcium absorption and bone mineralization, and has downstream effects on immune function, muscle function, and cellular signaling. The active form (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D) is produced in the kidneys; circulating 25(OH)D is the standard storage marker measured in labs.

## Evidence
- **NIH Office of Dietary Supplements — Vitamin D Fact Sheet for Health Professionals.** Reference on physiological roles, reference ranges, UL. https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/
- **Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline (2011, Holick et al.)** — Evaluation, Treatment, and Prevention of Vitamin D Deficiency. Recommends 25(OH)D target 30–100 ng/mL for at-risk adults; 1,500–2,000 IU/day commonly needed to maintain >30 ng/mL. Strong guideline.
- **Institute of Medicine (2011) Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D.** Sets RDA (600–800 IU) and UL (4,000 IU/day adults). More conservative than Endocrine Society.
- Note the divergence between IOM and Endocrine Society — the user's physician should choose a target informed by the user's labs.

## Parameters
- **Form:** D3 (cholecalciferol) > D2 (ergocalciferol) for raising blood levels.
- **Dose (adult, deficient):** Typical maintenance 1,000–4,000 IU/day with monitoring. Higher repletion doses (e.g. 50,000 IU weekly for 8 weeks) are used under physician supervision.
- **Timing:** With a meal containing fat for better absorption.
- **Monitoring:** Recheck 25(OH)D after 8–12 weeks of a new dose.
- **UL (adults):** 4,000 IU/day per IOM; some guidelines permit higher under supervision.

## Who this does NOT apply to
- Anyone with hypercalcemia, granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis), or primary hyperparathyroidism — vitamin D supplementation can worsen these.
- Anyone taking thiazide diuretics or digoxin should coordinate with physician.
- Pregnancy/lactation — use obstetric guidance.
- Children — different dosing.

## Interactions
- Thiazide diuretics (calcium retention).
- Some anticonvulsants, glucocorticoids, and weight-loss drugs can affect vitamin D metabolism — adjust dose with physician.
