# Magnesium Glycinate

## Claim
Adults with low dietary magnesium or symptoms consistent with low magnesium status (poor sleep quality, muscle cramps, low mood, constipation) may benefit from 200–400 mg/day of supplemental elemental magnesium, with glycinate (or bisglycinate) being a well-tolerated form for sleep and mood use-cases.

## Mechanism
Magnesium is a cofactor for ~300 enzymes, including those in ATP production, muscle contraction/relaxation, GABA receptor function, and melatonin synthesis. Most US/EU adults consume below the RDA. Glycine as the conjugate amino acid is itself a mild sleep-supporting molecule.

## Evidence
- **NIH Office of Dietary Supplements — Magnesium Fact Sheet for Health Professionals.** Reference for RDA (400–420 mg men / 310–320 mg women), UL from supplements (350 mg/day adults), dietary sources. https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Magnesium-HealthProfessional/
- **Abbasi et al. 2012** — RCT in elderly adults with insomnia: 500 mg magnesium (as magnesium oxide) for 8 weeks improved sleep latency and serum melatonin. Small (N=46). Emerging evidence.
- **Boyle et al. 2017** — systematic review on magnesium and subjective anxiety: evidence suggestive but limited by heterogeneity and study quality.
- **Barbagallo & Dominguez 2015** — review on magnesium in aging and chronic disease. Mechanistic rationale for supplementation in older adults.

Evidence tier: **emerging** for sleep/mood. **Strong** for repletion of actual deficiency (serum Mg <0.75 mmol/L) under physician direction.

## Parameters
- **Forms compared:** Glycinate / bisglycinate (well absorbed, minimal laxative effect, good for sleep). Citrate (well absorbed, mild laxative — useful if constipation is present). Oxide (poor absorption). Threonate (proposed CNS penetration — evidence thin).
- **Dose:** 200–400 mg elemental Mg/day. Start at 200 mg and titrate.
- **Timing:** 1–2 hours before bed for sleep use. With food to reduce GI effects.
- **UL (supplemental, adults):** 350 mg/day — this is for the supplemental amount, not total including food. Renal-impaired individuals need lower doses.

## Who this does NOT apply to
- Anyone with renal impairment (eGFR <60) — magnesium is renally cleared; risk of hypermagnesemia.
- Myasthenia gravis.
- Severe bradycardia or heart block.

## Interactions
- Oral bisphosphonates, tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones — separate by ≥2 hours (chelation reduces absorption).
- Thyroid medication (levothyroxine) — separate by ≥4 hours.
- Proton pump inhibitors (long-term PPI use depletes Mg — supplementation may be particularly relevant here).
- Diuretics (loop + thiazide deplete Mg; K-sparing can raise it).
