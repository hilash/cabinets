# Morning Light Exposure

## Claim
Brief (5–15 minute) exposure to bright natural light within the first 30–60 minutes of wake advances circadian phase, reduces sleep-onset latency at night, and increases daytime alertness. For most adults, this is one of the highest-leverage, lowest-cost behavioral levers for sleep quality.

## Mechanism
Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) containing melanopsin project to the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). Morning bright light exposure suppresses residual melatonin, triggers cortisol awakening response, and phase-advances the circadian pacemaker. The SCN in turn regulates peripheral clocks across metabolism, immunity, and mood.

## Evidence
- **Wright et al. 2013 (Current Biology)** — shifting to a camping schedule (natural light, no artificial light) advanced circadian timing by ~2 hours in one week. Demonstrates light is the dominant zeitgeber.
- **Münch et al. 2017 (Neuropsychobiology)** — review of light therapy in mood disorders; morning bright light shows efficacy in seasonal and non-seasonal depression.
- **Blume, Garbazza & Spitschan 2019 (Somnologie)** — review of effects of light on circadian rhythms, sleep, and mood in humans. Good overview of dose-response.
- **Khalsa et al. 2003 (J Physiol)** — phase-response curve to bright light in humans: morning light advances the clock, evening light delays it.

## Parameters
- **Timing:** Within 30–60 minutes of waking is ideal. Earlier better than later.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes on a sunny morning; 15–30 minutes on overcast days.
- **Intensity:** Outdoor natural light is orders of magnitude brighter than indoor lighting, even on overcast days. Through a window is weaker but still useful if outdoors isn't possible.
- **Do not stare at the sun.** Ambient exposure is sufficient — no direct gaze.
- **Glasses/contacts:** Fine. Sunglasses defeat the purpose for this protocol.
- **Paired behaviors:** Often stacked with light walk (movement + light + morning sunlight exposure compounds benefit).

## Who this does NOT apply to
- Individuals with photosensitive conditions (e.g. lupus, certain medications increasing photosensitivity — tetracyclines, amiodarone, some diuretics) — consult physician.
- Severe macular disease or recent eye surgery — defer to ophthalmologist.
- Bipolar disorder — morning bright light can be a phase trigger in some patients; proceed with psychiatric coordination.

## Contraindications in evening (complement)
- Avoid bright overhead light in the hour before intended sleep; prefer dim, warm-spectrum lighting. Evening light delays the circadian clock — the opposite of the morning effect.
