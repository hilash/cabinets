# 💡 Lighting

The single most under-rated production upgrade. A $100 light + a phone beats a $3K cinema camera in a dark room.

## The three-point setup (the only one you need to learn)

```
                  KEY (main light, 45° to subject, slightly above eye level)
                     │
                     ▼
                ╔═══════╗
                ║ YOU   ║
                ╚═══════╝
              ◀─ FILL  ─▶ HAIR / RIM (behind subject, separates from background)
              (opposite key, lower power, fills shadows)
```

- **Key** — the main light. ~70% of the brightness.
- **Fill** — opposite the key, ~30% as bright. Softens the shadow side.
- **Hair / rim** — behind the subject, pointing at the back of their head. Separates from the background. Single biggest "looks pro" upgrade.

## What to buy

| Tier | Setup |
|---|---|
| **Free** | Window light. Sit facing a window, mid-morning or late afternoon. Cheap white sheet over the window if it's harsh. |
| **$80** | One LED panel (Neewer, Aputure Amaran 60D used). Use as key. Bounce off a wall for fill. |
| **$300** | Two softbox kits (key + fill) + a small light for the hair light. |
| **$800+** | Aputure 200x or Godox SL-150W key + fill + practicals (lamps in the background). |

## Window light is genuinely good

The cheapest "studio quality" light you can use. Rules:

1. Face the window. Camera between you and the window. Never *behind* the window — you'll be a silhouette.
2. Soft window (north-facing, or with a sheer curtain) is better than direct sun.
3. Mid-morning to late afternoon. Avoid the 11am–2pm overhead-sun window.
4. If the room is too dark on the fill side, hang a white sheet on the wall opposite the window. Free fill.

## Common mistakes

- **Overhead office lights** — kills your face shadows, makes you look tired and waxy. Turn them off.
- **Light directly in front of the camera** — flat, lifeless, ring-light syndrome.
- **Mixed color temperature** — daylight from the window + warm tungsten lamp = green skin. Match all lights to the same color temp (~5500K is daylight).
- **Light too far away** — soft light gets harder the further it gets. Keep your softbox close.

## Practical lights (the secret weapon)

Lamps. Floor lamps. String lights. Edison bulbs. Neon signs. **The lights you can see in the frame** add depth and mood for almost free. Every "vibey" YouTube setup has 2–4 practicals in shot.

Buy warm-tinted (2700K) bulbs for these. Mismatch with your key color temp on purpose for warmth.

## Quick fix if you can't change your space

- Soft box light at face height, 45°.
- Position yourself further from the back wall (3–6 feet of separation).
- Add ANY light source on the back wall — even a $15 floor lamp.

That's 80% of "looks like a real YouTube channel."
