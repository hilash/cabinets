# Support Voice Guide

## Brand voice: Direct, warm, competent

Our support voice sounds like your smartest, most patient colleague — the one who explains things clearly the first time and never makes you feel foolish for asking.

## Tone principles

**DO:**
- Get to the problem immediately — acknowledge what they described specifically, not generically
- Use plain language: "click" not "navigate to", "turn on" not "enable the functionality"
- Own problems directly: "There's a bug affecting this — here's what we know and what's next"
- Offer a concrete next step every time — never end with "please let us know if you have questions"

**DO NOT:**
- "As per my previous email / our documentation / my last message"
- "Please be advised that..."
- "We apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused" (without then fixing it)
- Passive voice for bad news: not "an error was encountered" but "we have a bug that caused this"
- Corporate jargon: "leverage", "synergy", "circle back", "touch base", "reach out"

## Formality scale

| Customer tier | Opening | Closing |
|---|---|---|
| Enterprise | "Hi [Name]," | "Best, [Agent]" |
| Professional | "Hi [Name]," | "Thanks, [Agent]" |
| Starter / Trial | "Hey [Name]!" | "Let me know, [Agent]" |

## Structure every macro uses
1. **Acknowledge** — show you read their specific situation (1 sentence)
2. **Answer** — the clearest possible resolution or next step (numbered if multi-step)
3. **Link** — to the relevant help article if one exists
4. **Invite** — offer follow-up or escalation (1 sentence)

## Things that get macros flagged in QA
- Referencing a UI element that has been renamed
- Linking to a 404 article or an article with stale instructions
- Generic opening that could apply to any ticket ("Thank you for reaching out...")
- Missing a concrete next step
- Suggesting a workaround that no longer applies post-fix
