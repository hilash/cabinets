# ✒️ Writing Guide

Less about craft. More about the practical mechanics of finishing a book — without giving up in the messy middle.

## The single most important rule

**Drafts come before research. Always.**

The trap: you sit down to write chapter 4. You realize you don't know enough about X. You spend the writing session researching X. You feel productive. Six months later, you have 200 hours of research and 2 chapters drafted.

Reverse it. Sit down. Write what you know. Mark `[TK]` everywhere you don't. Research only what the draft proves you need.

## Daily practice

| Pattern | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| **Same time** | A specific window (6am, lunch break, after-kids) protected as writing time. The brain shows up faster on a schedule. |
| **Same place** | One desk, one chair, one set of headphones. The brain associates location with mode. |
| **Word count target, not time** | "1,000 words today" beats "I'll write for an hour". Time-based sessions become research sessions. |
| **No editing during drafting** | Forward only. Revisit tomorrow. The internal editor and the internal writer don't fit in the same room. |
| **Stop mid-sentence** | End the day mid-sentence (Hemingway's trick). Tomorrow's start is frictionless. |

## The minimum viable schedule

| Stage | Realistic target |
|---|---|
| Outline + premise sheet | 2 weeks of evenings |
| First draft | 6 to 12 months at 1,000 words/week |
| Structural revision | 6 to 10 weeks |
| Line + polish revision | 4 to 8 weeks |
| Cover + production + proofs | 4 to 8 weeks |
| TOTAL | 12 to 24 months |

Books take longer than first-time authors expect. Plan for 24, ship at 18, beat the budget.

## The messy middle

Chapters 4–8 are where most books die.

The pattern:
- Chapter 1–3 felt great (you wrote them on momentum)
- Chapter 4 stalls (the outline didn't account for what you've learned)
- You consider rewriting the whole outline
- You consider quitting

What to do:

1. **Skip the stuck chapter.** Write chapter 5 or 6 instead. Come back to 4 later. The stuck chapter is often stuck because it depends on something you haven't drafted yet.
2. **Talk it out.** Record yourself explaining the chapter to a friend. Transcribe. That's your draft.
3. **Lower the bar.** Aim for "bad first draft" not "good chapter". Bad drafts are revisable. Empty pages aren't.
4. **Re-read the premise.** If you're stuck because the premise is wrong — confront it. Better to re-outline at month 6 than to push through and discover the book doesn't work at month 18.

## Beta readers

Use them. NOT during first draft (they kill momentum). After first draft + structural pass.

- 5–8 readers
- Mix of: the target reader (most important), 1–2 writers/editors, 1–2 outsiders
- Give them a structured ask: "What confused you? Where did you skim? What chapter felt unnecessary?"
- Ignore line-level feedback at this stage — you'll polish later
- Look for PATTERNS — if 3 readers flagged the same chapter, address it; if 1 reader had a complaint, ignore it

## Editor (when, who, how much)

After your own structural + line + polish passes — hire a professional copy editor / proofreader.

| Type | What they do | ~Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Developmental editor | Big-picture: structure, premise, audience fit | $1,500–5,000 |
| Line editor | Sentence-level: voice, flow, clarity | $1,000–3,000 |
| Copy editor | Grammar, consistency, fact-checking | $500–1,500 |
| Proofreader | Final typo / formatting pass | $300–800 |

For a self-published book, **at minimum** hire a copy editor before publishing. Skipping this is the single most visible amateur tell.

## Maintaining momentum

The agent runs a Monday progress check that asks one question: are you still drafting?

If yes — momentum is the answer.
If no — diagnose: stuck chapter? premise drift? life chaos?

Don't quit. Just adjust. Pause is not abandonment — but unmanaged pause becomes abandonment in 3 months.
