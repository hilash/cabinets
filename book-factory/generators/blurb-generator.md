# 📜 Blurb Generator

The back-cover blurb sells the book. So does the Amazon description. So does the elevator pitch. They're all the same idea at different lengths.

## Generator prompt

```
You are the Book Coach. Read brand/index.md.

Output four nested versions of the book description, each tighter than the last.

[1. THE 25-WORD ELEVATOR PITCH]

  One sentence. Used in: chance conversations, podcast intros, Twitter bios, agent
  query letters. Names: target reader + problem + the book's unique angle.

  Example shape: "[Book title] is for [specific reader] who [problem]. It [unique angle/
  approach] and shows them how to [outcome]."

  Constraint: under 25 words. Read aloud — must sound spoken, not written.

[2. THE 60-WORD HOOK]

  Used in: Amazon "About this book" first paragraph, social shares, blog promotion.

  Structure:
    - Sentence 1: the reader's pain in their voice
    - Sentence 2: the conventional answer that doesn't work
    - Sentence 3: this book's different take + outcome

  Constraint: under 60 words. Active verbs. No "this book is".

[3. THE 150-WORD BACK-COVER BLURB]

  Used in: physical book back cover, Amazon full description.

  Structure:
    - Paragraph 1 (40 words): hook the reader with their pain or with a provocative
      claim. Land on the WHY this book exists.
    - Paragraph 2 (70 words): the WHAT — what the book delivers. Three concrete
      promises (what they'll learn, see, or be able to do).
    - Paragraph 3 (40 words): WHO is this for + a memorable closing line that
      captures the spirit. End with: "If [specific reader description], this book
      is for you."

  Constraint: 150 words total. Read aloud.

[4. THE 400-WORD AMAZON LONG DESCRIPTION]

  Used in: Amazon expanded description, book launch landing page, sales emails.

  Structure:
    - Open with the 60-word hook (verbatim)
    - Section: "What you'll learn" — 5–7 bullet points, each starting with a verb
    - Section: "Who this is for" — 3–5 bullets in the form "If you're a [role/level]
      who [situation], this book will give you [specific value]"
    - Section: "Who this is NOT for" — 2–3 bullets that filter out wrong-fit readers.
      Counter-intuitively, this DRAMATICALLY improves conversion.
    - Closing paragraph: a short personal note from the author. Why this book exists.
      End with the elevator pitch.

  Constraint: 400 words total.

After all four, output:

[KEYWORD STRINGS]

  Five keyword phrases for Amazon's 7-keyword field. Long-tail beats single words.
  Examples: "career advice for new managers" beats "management".
```

## Read every version aloud

The 60-word hook and the 25-word elevator pitch are the most important. Read each aloud 5 times. They should feel spoken — not written. If they sound like back-cover marketing speak, rewrite.

## Test the elevator pitch

Use it in real conversations. Say it to 5 people who don't know your book. Ask them: "what do you think this book is about?" If their answer matches your premise — it works. If they're confused — it doesn't.

This test catches premise problems faster than any beta-reader feedback ever will.

## Blurbs (the OTHER kind — endorsements)

Once the manuscript is complete, request endorsements from authors / experts in your space. The "blurb" on the cover (e.g. *"This book changed how I think about X." — Famous Author*).

Process:
1. Send the manuscript (or a strong sample) to ~15 people you'd want endorsements from
2. Be specific in your ask: "Would you read it and, if it resonates, share a 1–2 sentence reaction we could put on the cover?"
3. Make it easy: write 2–3 draft blurbs they can edit if they're short on time
4. Expect ~30% response rate; ~10% will give a usable blurb
5. Place the strongest 2–3 on the front/back cover or first interior page

Solid endorsements move the needle on bookstore browse-conversion meaningfully. Don't skip.
