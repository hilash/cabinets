# 🏛️ Traditional Publishing

> The longest path. Most prestigious. Lowest royalty. Highest barrier. Right for some books, wrong for most non-fiction first books.

## Pick traditional if

- The book NEEDS the prestige (memoir, narrative non-fiction, literary, academic)
- You want the broadest distribution including foreign markets, awards eligibility, library placement
- You have a great agent or a strong path to one
- You can wait 18–36 months between deal and shelves
- You're okay with ~10–15% royalty in exchange for an advance + marketing support
- You're committed to writing more than one book — traditional is a multi-book career investment

## Don't pick traditional if

- This is a how-to / business / lead-generation book — self-publishing serves you better
- You can't wait the timeline
- You don't have a sufficient platform to pitch (10K+ engaged readers minimum for most non-fiction)
- You want creative control over cover, title, marketing

## The path

```
Manuscript or proposal
        ↓
Get an agent (3–18 months of querying)
        ↓
Agent submits to publishers (1–6 months)
        ↓
Offer (or many rejections; back to step 2)
        ↓
Sign contract (1–3 months)
        ↓
Editing, design, production (12–18 months)
        ↓
Publication
```

Total: 18–36 months from manuscript-ready to in-stores.

## The book proposal

For non-fiction, you don't sell a finished manuscript — you sell a **book proposal**. ~30–50 pages including:

1. Overview (the premise, the hook, why this book now)
2. Target audience (size, demographics, evidence of demand)
3. Author bio + platform (your audience, your credibility)
4. Competitive title analysis (3–5 books, how yours is different)
5. Marketing plan (what YOU will do — publishers expect you to drive ~80% of marketing)
6. Chapter outline (1 paragraph per chapter)
7. Two sample chapters (your strongest, fully drafted)

The proposal is harder to write than the actual book. Plan 2–3 months of focused work.

## Getting an agent

Agents are gatekeepers for the Big 5 (Penguin Random House, HarperCollins, Simon & Schuster, Hachette, Macmillan) and most mid-size publishers.

How to find them:
- **Publishers Marketplace** — paid subscription, lists deals + agents who sold them
- **QueryTracker** — free, comprehensive agent database
- **Manuscript Wish List** — agents post what they're seeking
- **Twitter / X** — many agents are active; participate in #MSWL hashtag events
- **Conferences** — Writers' Digest, BookExpo, Publishing Industry events
- **Referrals** — if you know any traditionally-published authors, the warm intro is invaluable

Query letter best practices:
- Personalized (not "Dear Agent")
- One paragraph: hook + premise + comp titles
- One paragraph: bio + platform
- Sample as requested (often 5–10 pages or sample chapter)

Expect 50–100 queries. Expect 80%+ rejection. The right agent for your book exists, but finding them is a numbers game.

## The advance

If a publisher buys your book, they pay an "advance against royalties":
- First-time non-fiction non-celebrity advance: $5,000–25,000 typical, can be $50K+ for strong proposals with platform
- Paid in installments: signing → manuscript delivery → publication
- Royalties don't pay out until the advance is "earned out" — which most books never do (~70% of traditionally-published books never earn out their advance)

So: traditional isn't necessarily more lucrative than self-publishing. It depends entirely on the book and the audience.

## What the publisher does

- Editorial: developmental editor + copy editor (often great, sometimes harmful)
- Design: cover, interior, ARCs (advance reader copies)
- Production: print + ebook + audiobook
- Distribution: warehouses, returns processing, sales reps
- Marketing: ARC distribution to reviewers, some publicity, modest budgets
- Sales: publishing reps pitching to bookstores

## What the publisher does NOT do (despite what you might assume)

- Most marketing — that's still you, ~80% of the time
- Author tours — almost never funded for first-time authors
- TV / podcast bookings — that's you or your hired publicist
- Ongoing promotion — most books get a 6-week launch push and then are on their own

## When traditional is genuinely the right path

- Memoir or literary non-fiction (where prestige matters more than royalty)
- Big-idea books (the kind that need a publisher's reach to break out)
- Authors with platform (10K+ engaged) writing on a topic with a real "shelf" in bookstores
- Career writers who want a multi-book imprint relationship

For most other non-fiction first books — self-publishing is the right path. You'll earn more, ship faster, and own the rights. You can always go traditional with book 2.
