# Feeding cues before crying

> Crying is a *late* hunger cue. The earlier you catch a feed, the calmer the feed. Here's the cue ladder.

## The cue ladder (early → late)

### 1. Stirring (earliest)
Baby starts to come out of sleep — small movements, stretching, eyelid flutter, sometimes a soft sigh. If you offer a feed here, you get the calmest possible session: latched easily, deep swallows, no crying to interrupt the flow.

### 2. Mouthing / rooting
Head turns side to side. Mouth opens. Tongue may protrude. The "rooting reflex" — they're searching. Often you'll see them turn toward anything that touches the cheek (breast, finger, your shirt).

### 3. Hands to mouth
Baby brings their fists up, sucks on knuckles, gnaws on a thumb. Strong cue. Still calm — still a great feed window.

### 4. Vocalizing
Soft fussing sounds. Not a full cry. Higher pitch than usual, shorter bursts.

### 5. Crying (latest)
The cue you don't want to wait for. By the time a newborn is full-out crying, they're flooded with stress hormones — harder to latch, harder to coordinate suck-swallow-breathe, more likely to swallow air (gas), and you'll be working through their distress before any feeding actually happens.

## What this means in practice

- **Feed at stirring or rooting.** That's the sweet spot.
- **If they're crying:** calm them first (skin-to-skin, swaddle, dim room) for 30–60 seconds, then offer the feed. Trying to latch a crying baby usually fails twice before it works.
- **Don't watch the clock to start a feed** — watch the cues. Newborns cluster, growth spurt, cue irregularly. The "every 3 hours" thing is an average, not a schedule.
- **Do watch the clock between feeds at night** — if it's been 3+ hours and they're still asleep, gently wake to feed (especially in the first few weeks when weight gain is the priority).

## What's not a hunger cue

- **Crying that started 5 seconds ago in a swaddled baby who just ate** is more likely positioning, gas, or temperature than hunger.
- **Sucking on hands** — newborns also self-soothe by sucking. If they're calm, alert, and just had a feed 30 minutes ago, the sucking is exploration, not hunger.
- **Wanting to suck but not actually drawing milk after 2 minutes at the breast** — that's comfort sucking. Different need. Maybe the pacifier is what's wanted (after breastfeeding is established).

## Sleepy feeders

Some newborns — especially in the first 2–3 weeks, or after jaundice, or if a little early — fall asleep within 5 minutes of latching. Tactics:

- Strip down to a diaper to keep them awake (cool air on skin)
- Switch sides often (every 2–3 minutes if they keep dozing)
- Compress the breast gently to keep milk flowing — they'll wake to swallow
- Tickle feet, change diaper mid-feed, sing to them

If sleepy feeding is consistent and weight gain is a concern, mention it to the pediatrician or an IBCLC. Sometimes it's a tongue tie (see [[tongue-tie-signs|tongue tie signs]]). Sometimes it's just the early weeks.

## When you can let a baby sleep through a feed

After 2–4 weeks, once they've regained birth weight and are tracking on the growth chart, most pediatricians say: "let them sleep, they'll wake when they're hungry." Confirm this with your own pediatrician at the 2-week visit. Until then: cue-feed during the day, and don't let stretches go past 3 hours overnight.
