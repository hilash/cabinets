# Fever rules — under 3 months is different

> Read this once. Tell your partner. Pin it.

## The rule

**Any rectal temperature ≥ 100.4°F (38°C) in a baby under 3 months old is a medical emergency. Go to the ER, not the pediatrician.**

This is non-negotiable. It is not a "wait and see." It is not "give Tylenol and call in the morning." It is the ER, immediately, every time.

## Why this rule exists

Newborns have an immature immune system. A fever in this age group can be the *only* sign of a serious bacterial infection — sepsis, meningitis, urinary tract infection, pneumonia. By the time other symptoms show up, hours of intervention have been lost. The standard ER workup for a febrile under-3-month-old includes blood, urine, and often spinal-fluid tests. That's the standard of care because catching the rare bad outcome justifies the inconvenience of the common false alarm.

Pediatricians' offices know this rule too. If you call the after-hours line for a febrile newborn, they will tell you to go to the ER.

## How to take a temperature in a newborn

**Rectal is the standard.** Forehead and ear thermometers are not accurate enough at this age to make ER-or-not decisions on. Armpit temps are estimates.

- Use a digital rectal thermometer with a flexible tip
- A small amount of petroleum jelly on the tip
- Insert no more than ½ inch (about 1.25 cm)
- Hold baby still on their back with knees bent toward chest, or on their side
- Wait for the beep (usually 10–30 seconds)

If the rectal reading is **≥ 100.4°F (38°C)**: ER.
If it reads 99.something but baby seems off, take it again in 30 minutes. If still 99-something but you're worried, call the nurse line.

## Once baby is over 3 months

The rule changes:

- **3–6 months:** Fever ≥ 102°F (38.9°C), or any fever lasting >24 hours, or a fever with other concerning symptoms → call pediatrician
- **6 months and up:** Fever in itself is less alarming. Look at the whole picture: how is the baby acting, are they drinking fluids, are they urinating normally, is there a rash, is there respiratory distress?

But until that 3-month mark: **100.4°F = ER.**

## What about Tylenol/acetaminophen?

**Do not give Tylenol to a baby under 3 months without ER/pediatrician guidance.** It can mask the fever and complicate the workup. The ER is the right call. Bring the baby exactly as they are.

If your pediatrician has given you a specific weight-based dose for fever in a baby over 2 months (some give this for post-vaccine fevers), follow that — but post-vaccine fever guidance is not the same as "any fever."

## What to bring to the ER

- The baby's rectal thermometer reading and the time you took it
- Their birth weight, current weight if you know it, and any feeding/diaper history from the last 24 hours
- Vaccination card or printout
- Insurance card
- A blanket, a pacifier if used, a change of diaper or two
- Phone charger — these workups take hours

## After

The ER will likely admit for observation, even if early tests come back fine. That's standard. Most under-3-month fevers turn out to be viral and resolve, but the workup is the workup. Don't take the admission as a sign something terrible is happening — take it as a sign the system is doing exactly what it should.
