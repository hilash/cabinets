# Cluster feeding — why and what to do

> If your baby wants to feed every 30–60 minutes for several hours in a row — usually in the late afternoon or evening — that's cluster feeding. It's normal. It does not mean you don't have enough milk.

## What it is

Three or more feeds within roughly three hours, often back-to-back, sometimes with the baby barely "off" before wanting back "on." Most common 4pm–8pm but can happen any time. Peaks during the witching hour and around growth spurts.

## When it happens (the predictable times)

- **First few days**: cluster feeding helps establish supply. Your body reads frequency as demand.
- **Days 7–10**: first growth spurt
- **Weeks 2–3**: another growth spurt
- **Weeks 4–6**: another
- **Weeks 8–12**: longer growth spurts
- **3 months, 6 months, 9 months**: developmental leaps that often come with appetite spikes
- **Daily evening cluster** (the witching-hour variety): often 4pm–8pm, every day, until ~3 months

## Why it happens

For breastfeeding babies:
- **Loading the tank for a longer stretch.** Cluster feeding before bed often correlates with the longer overnight sleep.
- **Stimulating supply during a growth spurt.** Frequency drives prolactin drives supply. Within 1–3 days, supply usually catches up.
- **Comfort.** Especially during the witching hour, sucking is regulating, not just nutritive.

For bottle-fed babies:
- Same pattern can show up — same biology, different delivery
- Cluster bottle-feeding can mean smaller, more frequent feeds, not necessarily larger total volume

## What it does NOT mean

- **You don't have enough milk.** Cluster feeding is a normal part of how supply *increases*, not a sign that it's failing.
- **The baby is starving.** A baby who's gaining weight, has 6+ wet diapers a day, and is otherwise fine is fed.
- **Your formula is wrong.** Switching brands during a cluster phase rarely changes anything except your stress.
- **You should pump and "see how much is in there."** Pump output is not a measure of breast milk supply. Babies extract more efficiently than pumps.

## What to actually do

- **Set up your station.** Water, a snack, charged phone, something to watch or read, the remote, a blanket, the baby. Once you sit down, you're sitting down.
- **Switch nurse.** If breastfeeding, offer the second side every time the baby pulls off. Then back to the first. They'll let you know when they're done.
- **Hand off to your partner** between feeds. They burp, change, walk, soothe. You hydrate.
- **Don't watch the clock.** Cluster feeds end when they end. Watching the clock makes the hour feel like three.
- **If bottle feeding:** offer paced feeds (see [[paced-bottle-feeding|paced bottle feeding]]) so the baby doesn't get overfed during a comfort-driven cluster.

## When it's actually a supply concern

Look at the baby, not the feeding pattern:

- **Wet diapers**: 6+ in 24 hours past day 5 = hydrated
- **Weight**: regaining birth weight by 2 weeks, then ~5–7 oz/week for the first few months
- **Behavior**: alert when awake, settling between feeds (eventually), responsive

If wet diapers drop below 6/day, weight gain stalls past the 2-week mark, or the baby is consistently lethargic — *that's* when to call the pediatrician or an IBCLC. Cluster feeding alone is not.

## The thing nobody tells you

Cluster feeding feels endless because it's relentless and usually overlaps with the most exhausted part of your day. It also predictably ends — daily clusters ease around 12 weeks, growth-spurt clusters last 24–72 hours and then taper. Mark them on the calendar so you can see the pattern. Patterns are easier to live through than mystery.
