# Witching hour & PURPLE crying

> If your baby is fussy, crying, cluster-feeding, and inconsolable from roughly 5pm to 10pm — **nothing is wrong with your baby. Nothing is wrong with you.** This is universal. Here's what's happening and what helps.

## What it is

The witching hour (a misnomer — it's usually 3–5 hours, not 1) typically appears around 2–3 weeks old, peaks around 6–8 weeks, and starts to ease by 3–4 months. Theories for why:

- **Cumulative overstimulation.** Newborns can't filter sensory input the way you can. By evening, the day has been too much.
- **Gut development.** The microbiome is establishing. Gas, fussiness, and pulling-up-of-knees are common around this time.
- **Pre-sleep cluster-feeding.** Babies often want to top off the tank before the longer overnight stretch.
- **Mom's milk supply naturally dips slightly in the evening.** Breastfed babies may need to feed more often in shorter intervals.
- **Catecholamine wash.** Stress hormones from a full day need to discharge somehow. Sometimes that's crying.

## PURPLE crying

Dr. Ronald Barr coined "PURPLE crying" to describe the *normal* crying pattern of newborns:

- **P**eak around 6–8 weeks
- **U**nexpected — comes and goes for no clear reason
- **R**esists soothing — nothing fully works, and that's expected
- **P**ain-like face — but not in pain
- **L**ong-lasting — bouts can run hours
- **E**vening — typically late afternoon and evening

This is different from colic in that it's universal — every healthy baby goes through some version of it. "Colic" is usually defined as crying more than 3 hours a day, more than 3 days a week, for more than 3 weeks. Most witching-hour babies don't meet that bar.

## What helps (rotate, don't expect any one thing to work every time)

- **The 5 S's** — Swaddle, Side/stomach position (only while held and awake), Shush (white noise loud enough to be heard over the crying), Swing (rhythmic motion), Suck (pacifier or breast)
- **Skin-to-skin** in a dim room, even if the crying continues
- **Babywearing** — strap baby on, walk outside if you can. Motion + closeness + warmth + the sound of your heartbeat regulates them.
- **Warm bath**, often with the baby on you
- **Hand-off to your partner** if anyone is around — not because you're failing, because a fresh person calms a fresh baby
- **Lower the lights, lower the volume**, no visitors during this window
- **Cluster-feed if she'll take it.** It's normal. Hydrate yourself, snack, push through.

## What does NOT help

- **Trying to "schedule" out of it.** This window is biologically driven — it ends when development moves on, not when you optimize.
- **Switching formulas every other day** trying to find "the one." Unless your pediatrician suspects a true allergy or intolerance (rare), the formula isn't the problem.
- **Driving in the car for hours.** Many parents do it. It works in the moment. It also normalizes the only-sleeps-in-the-car pattern. Use sparingly.
- **Listening to anyone who tells you "back in my day, my baby slept through from week 2."** Selective memory.

## When witching-hour crying becomes "call the doctor"

- Crying inconsolably for **3+ hours** straight despite all of the above
- Crying combined with **fever**, vomiting, refusing feeds, or a swollen/firm belly
- Crying that has changed character — higher-pitched, weaker, or with limpness in between
- Anything that feels different from the usual evening fuss

See [[red-flags|Red flags — when to call the doctor]].

## The thing nobody tells you

It ends. Around 3–4 months for most babies, the witching hour eases dramatically. You will not believe it while you're in it. You will not remember how bad it felt once it's gone. That's also normal.
