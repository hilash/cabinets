Motion

Position · velocity · acceleration · the language of how things move
complete ~ 90 min

Drop a tennis ball from your hand. It hits the floor in about half a second. In that half second, it covered roughly a meter — but it didn't cover the meter at a steady pace. It started slow and finished fast.

That single observation contains the three ideas that run this entire module: position (where the ball is), velocity (how fast its position is changing), and acceleration (how fast its velocity is changing). If you can tell those three apart, you can describe the motion of anything.

Position (x)

Position is where something is, measured from a chosen origin. Units: meters (m). It's a number with a sign — pick "right" as positive and x = +3 m means three meters to the right; x = -2 m means two to the left. The origin is your choice; pick whatever makes the problem easy.

Displacement (Δx)

Displacement is the change in position: Δx = x_final − x_initial. Not the same as distance traveled. Walk 3 m east then 3 m back west: distance = 6 m, displacement = 0 m. Physics cares about displacement.

Velocity (v)

Velocity is how fast position changes, and in what direction. Units: meters per second (m/s).

v_avg = Δx / Δt = (x_final − x_initial) / (t_final − t_initial)

Velocity has a sign: +5 m/s means moving in the positive direction at 5 m/s; -5 m/s means the same speed in the opposite direction. Speed is just the magnitude of velocity.

Acceleration (a)

Acceleration is how fast velocity changes. Units: meters per second per second (m/s²).

a_avg = Δv / Δt = (v_final − v_initial) / (t_final − t_initial)

Three things to internalize:

  1. Acceleration is not "going fast." A car cruising at 100 km/h has zero acceleration.
  2. Same sign as velocity → speeding up. Opposite sign → slowing down ("deceleration" is just a word — physics only has acceleration).
  3. On Earth, free-falling objects have a downward acceleration of g ≈ 9.8 m/s², regardless of mass.

The four kinematic equations (constant acceleration)

For straight-line motion with constant acceleration, four equations cover almost everything:

1. v = v₀ + a·t (velocity at time t) 2. x = x₀ + v₀·t + ½·a·t² (position at time t) 3. v² = v₀² + 2·a·(x − x₀) (velocity vs position, no t) 4. x = x₀ + ½·(v₀ + v)·t (uses average velocity)

v₀ is initial velocity, x₀ is initial position. Pick the equation whose unknowns match what you have and want.

Graphs of motion

Two patterns to memorize: a straight, sloped line on x-t = constant velocity. A straight, sloped line on v-t = constant acceleration (which makes x-t a parabola).

Problem. A car is stopped at a red light. The light turns green and the car accelerates at a constant 2.5 m/s² for 8 seconds, then continues at that velocity. How far has the car traveled at the moment it stops accelerating, and how fast is it going?

Step 1 — what we know

v₀ = 0 m/s (starts from rest) a = 2.5 m/s² t = 8 s x₀ = 0 m (call the stoplight the origin)

Step 2 — pick equations

For final velocity, equation 1: v = v₀ + a·t = 0 + (2.5)(8) = 20 m/s.

For position, equation 2: x = x₀ + v₀·t + ½·a·t² = 0 + 0 + ½·(2.5)·(64) = 80 m.

Step 3 — sanity check

Average velocity should be (0 + 20)/2 = 10 m/s. Multiplied by 8 s gives 80 m. ✓

Answer: The car has traveled 80 meters and is moving at 20 m/s (~72 km/h, or 45 mph) when it stops accelerating.

Try each one with paper before peeking.

Q1.A jogger runs 200 m east in 50 s, pauses for 10 s, then walks 50 m east in 25 s. What is her average velocity over the entire trip?
Show answer
Total displacement = 200 + 0 + 50 = 250 m east. Total time = 50 + 10 + 25 = 85 s. v_avg = 250 / 85 ≈ 2.94 m/s east.
Q2.A skateboarder is rolling at +4 m/s when she starts pushing, accelerating at +0.5 m/s² for 6 s. What is her velocity at the end of the 6 s?
Show answer
v = v₀ + a·t = 4 + (0.5)(6) = 4 + 3 = 7 m/s.
Q3.A ball is dropped from rest off a bridge and falls for 3 s before hitting the water. Using g = 9.8 m/s², how fast is it moving when it hits, and how far did it fall?
Show answer
v = v₀ + g·t = 0 + (9.8)(3) = 29.4 m/s downward. Distance: x = ½·g·t² = ½·(9.8)·(9) = 44.1 m.
Q4.A cyclist traveling at +12 m/s brakes uniformly and comes to a stop in 4 s. What is the acceleration (include sign), and how far does the bike travel during braking?
Show answer
a = (v − v₀)/t = (0 − 12)/4 = -3 m/s² (negative because she's slowing down). Distance: average velocity is (12 + 0)/2 = 6 m/s, times 4 s = 24 m.
Q5.Looking at a velocity-vs-time graph, you see a straight horizontal line at v = -3 m/s from t = 0 to t = 6 s. Describe the motion in words, and find the displacement over those 6 s.
Show answer
The object is moving in the negative direction at a constant 3 m/s — no acceleration, just steady backward motion. Displacement is the area under the v-t graph: (-3 m/s)(6 s) = -18 m (i.e., 18 meters in the negative direction).

Every acceleration in this module had a cause — gravity, an engine, friction. That cause has a name: force. Module 2 introduces Newton's three laws and F = m·a.

Waves

Frequency · amplitude · interference · how disturbances travel
complete ~ 100 min

Pluck a guitar string. Drop a pebble in a pond. Yell across a canyon. Wave at a friend (literally). In every case, something is moving — but the string doesn't fly off, the pond water doesn't travel to shore, the air between you and your friend doesn't whoosh forward.

Energy is moving. The medium isn't. That single idea — energy traveling through stuff without the stuff going anywhere — is the whole game of waves.

What a wave actually is

A wave is a disturbance that travels through a medium (or through space), carrying energy without permanently moving the medium itself. Picture a row of dominoes — each one barely moves, but the falling travels all the way down the line.

The four numbers that describe any wave

QuantitySymbolWhat it measuresUnits
Wavelengthλ (lambda)Distance from one peak to the next peakmeters (m)
FrequencyfHow many full waves pass a point each secondhertz (Hz)
PeriodTTime for one full wave to passseconds (s)
AmplitudeAMax height of a peak from the resting linemeters or signal-units

Period and frequency are tied: T = 1 / f and f = 1 / T. If 5 waves pass per second (f = 5 Hz), each one takes 1/5 s (T = 0.2 s). Same fact, two ways.

Amplitude tells you how loud, bright, or strong the wave is. Frequency tells you what kind of wave it is — high pitch vs low pitch, red light vs blue light. They're independent: you can crank up volume without changing the note.

Wave speed

How fast does a wave move? Multiply how long each wave is by how many pass per second:

v = f · λ

Most important equation in this module. Memorize it. v is in m/s, f in Hz, λ in m. The medium sets the speed — frequency and wavelength trade off against each other inside that constant.

Transverse vs longitudinal

  • Transverse: the medium moves perpendicular to the wave's direction. Flick a rope up and down — it wiggles vertically while the wave travels horizontally. Examples: light, water surface waves, guitar strings.
  • Longitudinal: the medium moves parallel to the wave's direction — squeezing and stretching. Push and pull a slinky along its length. Examples: sound in air, P-waves in earthquakes.

Interference: what happens when waves meet

Two waves passing through the same spot at the same time add together — the superposition principle. You literally just add the heights at each point.

  • Constructive: peaks line up with peaks. Bigger wave. Louder, brighter, taller.
  • Destructive: peaks line up with troughs. They cancel. Silence, darkness, flatness.

Noise-cancelling headphones are pure destructive interference: the headphones listen to incoming sound, generate the inverted wave, and the two cancel inside your ear. After the meeting, both waves keep going as if nothing happened.

Problem. A tuning fork vibrates at 440 Hz (concert A). The sound wave travels through air at 343 m/s. What is the wavelength?

Step 1 — what we know

Frequency f = 440 Hz. Wave speed v = 343 m/s. Find: wavelength λ.

Step 2 — equation

The wave-speed equation: v = f · λ. Rearrange: λ = v / f.

Step 3 — plug in

λ = 343 m/s ÷ 440 Hz = 0.78 m

Step 4 — sanity check

About 78 cm — roughly the length of your arm. Reasonable for an audible sound (audible wavelengths range from a couple centimeters up to ~17 m). Higher pitch = shorter wavelength.

Answer: λ ≈ 0.78 m

5 · Practice5 questions · answers below each
›
Q1.A wave on a rope has a wavelength of 2 m and a frequency of 5 Hz. How fast is it traveling?
Show answer
v = f · λ = 5 Hz × 2 m = 10 m/s.
Q2.A radio station broadcasts at 100 MHz (10⁸ Hz). Radio waves travel at the speed of light, 3 × 10⁸ m/s. What's the wavelength?
Show answer
λ = v / f = (3 × 10⁸) / 10⁸ = 3 m. That's why FM radio antennas are about a meter long — they're tuned to a fraction of the wavelength.
Q3.You double the amplitude of a sound wave but leave the frequency alone. What changes — pitch, loudness, both, or neither?
Show answer
Loudness only. Amplitude controls intensity; frequency controls pitch. They're independent.
Q4.A pendulum completes one full swing every 0.5 seconds. What's its frequency?
Show answer
f = 1 / T = 1 / 0.5 = 2 Hz.
Q5.Two identical waves meet. Wave A's peak lines up exactly with Wave B's trough. What does the combined wave look like at that instant — and what is this called?
Show answer
The waves cancel completely — flat at the meeting point. Destructive interference. If they keep traveling, they emerge unchanged on the other side.

Bridge to Module 5 — Electricity. You just learned that waves carry energy through a medium without the medium going anywhere. Hold onto that. In Module 5 you'll meet current — the flow of electrons. At first it looks like the opposite of a wave: actual particles moving down a wire. But when you flip a light switch, the bulb lights up almost instantly, even if the wire is a kilometer long. The electrons themselves drift slowly. The energy travels as a wave — an electromagnetic disturbance racing down the wire near the speed of light. Same trick.

Electricity

Charge · current · voltage · resistance · the flow of electrons
complete ~ 100 min

Pick up a 9-volt battery (the rectangular kind with both terminals on top) and touch it to your tongue. You feel a sharp, metallic tingle. Touch the same battery to your fingertip — nothing.

Same battery. Same 9 volts. Why does your tongue feel it but your skin doesn't? The answer is hiding in this module — three numbers explain it: voltage, resistance, current.

Charge (Q)

Atoms contain protons (+) and electrons (−). More electrons than protons → negative charge. Fewer → positive. Equal → neutral. Symbol: Q. Unit: coulomb (C). One coulomb ≈ 6.24 × 10¹⁸ electrons. Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.

Current (I)

When charges move through a wire, you have a current — the rate of charge flow. Symbol: I. Unit: ampere (A) = 1 coulomb per second. Direction by convention: from + to − (Benjamin Franklin guessed wrong; we never bothered to flip). Think of it as flow rate of water in a pipe: liters per second of water → coulombs per second of charge.

Voltage (V)

Voltage is electrical pressure — energy per unit charge. Symbol: V. Unit: volt (V) = 1 joule per coulomb. A 9V battery puts 9 joules into every coulomb that flows out. Water analogy: the height drop between a tank and a tap.

Resistance (R)

Every wire and component fights the flow of charge. That opposition is resistance. Symbol: R. Unit: ohm (Ω). A short, thick copper wire: tiny resistance. A long, thin tungsten filament: big resistance — that's why it heats up and glows.

Ohm's Law

The most-used equation in electronics:

V = I × R

Rearrangements: I = V / R (current grows with voltage, shrinks with resistance) and R = V / I. Most of intro circuit analysis is bookkeeping around this one equation.

Series vs Parallel

Series — components on a single loop. Same current flows through every one. Resistances add:

R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …

Parallel — components on separate branches between the same two points. Same voltage across each branch. Currents add. Total resistance is less than the smallest individual resistance:

1 / R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + …

Intuition: parallel paths give the current more room to flow, like opening extra lanes on a highway.

3 · InteractiveReasoning Exercises
›

Reason out each scenario before peeking. Cover the answer with your hand if you have to.

A.The doubled battery — one 1.5V battery + one 10Ω resistor. You measure the current. Now you swap the battery for a 3V one (same resistor). What happens to the current?
Show answer
Current doubles. Ohm: I = V / R. R unchanged, V doubled, so I doubles. New: 3 / 10 = 0.3 A. Old: 1.5 / 10 = 0.15 A.
B.Two identical bulbs wired in series. Both glow dimly. You unscrew one. What happens to the other?
Show answer
Goes out. Series = same current through every component. Removing one bulb breaks the loop — current can't flow at all.
C.Same two bulbs, now wired in parallel to the same battery. You unscrew one. What happens to the other?
Show answer
Keeps glowing — and a touch brighter in real circuits. Each bulb is on its own branch with the full battery voltage across it. (This is why house wiring is parallel: turning off the kitchen light shouldn't kill the bedroom.)
D.Back to the hook: 9V on tongue (~5,000 Ω resistance) vs 9V on dry fingertip (~100,000 Ω). Estimate the current in each. Which can you feel?
Show answer
Tongue: I = 9 / 5,000 = 1.8 mA. You feel that. Fingertip: I = 9 / 100,000 = 0.09 mA. Below the threshold of sensation. Same voltage, different resistance, different current, different sensation.
4 · WorkedSeries circuit, two resistors
›

Problem. A 12V battery in series with a 4Ω resistor and an 8Ω resistor. Find: (1) total resistance, (2) current in the loop, (3) voltage across each resistor.

Step 1 — total resistance

Series add: R_total = 4 + 8 = 12 Ω.

Step 2 — current

The whole loop sees 12V across 12Ω. I = V / R_total = 12 / 12 = 1 A. Same 1 A through every component (defining property of series).

Step 3 — voltage per resistor

Apply Ohm to each (same current, different R):

  • V₁ = I × R₁ = 1 × 4 = 4 V
  • V₂ = I × R₂ = 1 × 8 = 8 V

Sanity check. The voltages add to the battery: 4 + 8 = 12 V ✓. This is Kirchhoff's voltage law in disguise: every joule a coulomb gains from the battery has to be spent somewhere in the loop.

5 · Practice5 questions · answers below each
›
Q1.A 6V battery and a 2Ω resistor. What is the current?
Show answer
I = V / R = 6 / 2 = 3 A.
Q2.A current of 0.5 A flows through a resistor when 10V is applied. What is its resistance?
Show answer
R = V / I = 10 / 0.5 = 20 Ω.
Q3.Three resistors — 2Ω, 3Ω, 5Ω — connected in series. Total resistance?
Show answer
R_total = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 Ω. Series resistances simply add.
Q4.Two 6Ω resistors in parallel. Total resistance?
Show answer
1 / R_total = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3, so R_total = 3 Ω. Shortcut for two equal resistors in parallel: halve the value. Two equal lanes carry twice the current.
Q5.A toaster on a 120V outlet draws 10 A. What's the resistance of the heating element, and how much charge flows through it in one minute?
Show answer
Resistance: R = V / I = 120 / 10 = 12 Ω. Charge in one minute: Q = I × t = 10 × 60 = 600 C — about 3.7 × 10²¹ electrons. Toast is hard work.

Bridge to Module 6 — Light. You just learned that moving charges = current. Here's the punchline that connects this module to the next: whenever charges accelerate, they radiate electromagnetic waves. The current wiggling in a radio antenna is what produces radio waves. Electrons jumping between energy levels in a lightbulb filament are what produces visible light. Electricity and light are not two phenomena — they're the same field, vibrating at different frequencies.
