1

Motion

Position · velocity · acceleration · the language of how things move
Drop a ball. It hits the floor in half a second, a meter down — but not at constant speed. Three ideas explain that: position, velocity, acceleration. If you can tell them apart, you can describe the motion of anything.
complete ~ 90 min
The hook: drop a tennis ball from your hand. Half a second. ~1 meter. Not at constant speed — slow at first, fast at the end. The whole module is hidden inside that one observation.

Forces

Newton's three laws · why things move (or don't)
Module 1 told you how things move. This one tells you why. Every acceleration has a cause — a force. Push, pull, gravity, friction. F = m·a is the equation that ties Module 1 to the physical world.

Energy

Kinetic · potential · conservation · the currency of physics
A roller-coaster sits at the top of the first hill, full of potential energy. It rushes through the loop, every joule of potential converting to kinetic and back, friction skimming a bit each cycle. Energy is conserved — the bookkeeping always balances.
stub ~ 90 min
This module is in stub state. The PhET Energy Skate Park sim below is the perfect interactive — drop a skater into the half-pipe and watch KE/PE swap in real time, with and without friction.

4

Waves

Frequency · amplitude · interference · how disturbances travel
Pluck a guitar string. Drop a pebble in a pond. Yell across a canyon. Each is energy traveling through stuff while the stuff doesn't go anywhere. Waves: the substrate of sound, light, earthquakes, and anything that wiggles.
complete ~ 100 min
The hook: the string doesn't fly off the guitar. The pond water doesn't travel to shore. The air between you and your friend doesn't whoosh forward. Energy is moving. The medium isn't.

5

Electricity

Charge · current · voltage · resistance — the flow of electrons
Touch a 9-volt battery to your tongue and you feel it. Touch the same battery to your fingertip — nothing. Same battery, same 9 volts. Three numbers tell you why: voltage, resistance, current. Ohm's law in three keystrokes.
complete ~ 100 min
The hook: a 9V battery on the tongue tingles. On a fingertip, nothing. Why? The answer is hiding in three numbers — voltage, resistance, current — and the law that ties them together.

Light

Reflection · refraction · the EM spectrum · waves and particles
A straw looks bent in a glass of water. The sky is blue at noon and red at sunset. A prism splits white light into a rainbow. Light is wave (Module 4 applies) and particle (quantization shows up). The final module bridges classical and quantum.
