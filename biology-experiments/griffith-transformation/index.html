About Griffith's experiment

Frederick Griffith worked with two forms of Streptococcus pneumoniae. Smooth S cells have a capsule that lets them evade host defenses, while rough R cells lack that capsule and are nonvirulent.

  • Live R: mouse lives, so R cells alone are not virulent.
  • Live S: mouse dies, and live S cells are recovered.
  • Heat-killed S: mouse lives, showing killed S cells cannot cause disease by themselves.
  • Live R + heat-killed S: mouse dies, and live S cells are recovered. A stable material from dead S cells transformed live R cells into heritable S cells.

Griffith called the unknown material the transforming principle. Later experiments by Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty identified that material as DNA.
