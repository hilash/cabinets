# Resume Optimization Guide

Your resume has about 6 seconds to make someone want to keep reading. Here's how to make those seconds count.

## The Golden Rules

1. **One page.** Unless you have 15+ years of experience and the role explicitly values depth.
2. **Lead with impact, not duties.** "Increased retention by 18%" beats "Responsible for customer retention."
3. **Mirror the job posting.** Use their language where it honestly applies. ATS systems match keywords.
4. **Quantify everything.** Revenue, users, percentages, team size, time saved, scope reduced.
5. **Most relevant experience first.** Reorder bullets so the strongest match is on top.

## The Anti-Patterns

- "Responsible for..." — passive, boring, says nothing about results
- "Assisted with..." — diminishes your contribution
- "Various stakeholders" — vague, name the function (engineering, sales, exec team)
- "Helped improve..." — by how much? When? What was the baseline?
- Listing technologies without context — "Python, SQL, Tableau" means nothing without impact

## ATS Survival Guide

Most companies use applicant tracking systems that filter resumes by keyword match before a human sees them.

- **Use the exact job title** if your actual title is close (PM vs Product Manager)
- **Include both acronyms and full forms** (OKR and Objectives and Key Results)
- **Use standard section headers** (Experience, Education, Skills — not "My Journey" or "What I've Built")
- **No tables, columns, or graphics** — ATS parsers struggle with complex layouts
- **PDF format** — unless they specifically ask for Word

## The Tailoring Process

For each application, the Resume Tailor agent will:

1. Extract keywords and requirements from the job posting
2. Rewrite your top 3-5 bullets to mirror their language
3. Reorder sections to lead with the most relevant experience
4. Flag any gaps you should address in a cover letter
5. Generate the tailored version as a new page in `/profile`

**The rule:** Change at most 40% of the resume per application. You should still sound like yourself.

## Cover Letters

Most people hate writing them. Most hiring managers skim them. But a good one can tip the scale.

**The 3-paragraph formula:**
1. Why this company and this role specifically (prove you researched them)
2. One concrete achievement that maps to their biggest need
3. A confident close with a specific call to action

**Skip the cover letter when:**
- The application doesn't ask for one
- You have a warm referral who can speak to your fit
- The company culture signals that they don't read them (check Glassdoor)
